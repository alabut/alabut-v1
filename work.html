Selection of work from the last two years

I've been working solely for Bay Area startups since March 2007. Startups let me wear all the different hats of an interaction designer (usability, design and programming) in a fast-paced environment with lots of experimentation. Below are highlights from several companies.

1. Asterpix asterpix.com

My role:

  1. video player UI design
  2. site and logo design
  3. usability tests and video tutorials

1. video player UI design

Asterpix is a startup with a very unique video player — it uses advanced object recognition algorithms so that you can attach notes to any object in a video. Designing a UI for this new “popup video” was challenging. How would we preserve the playback experience and make videos that people can watch like they're already used to, while also getting across that they can interact with them and do more than just watch?

After doing an extensive audit of competing niche video players and finding most of them to be overly complex, I finally found inspiration for the new functionality from the best non-video sites: Flickr, Google Maps, and Youtube.

original video player (May 2007)

2. site and logo design

Since the concept of watching and interacting with “hypervideos” was so new, the site itself needed to be as easy to use as possible and easily communicate this new type of interaction in layman's terms. Accomplishing these usability and learnability goals translated to two concrete graphic design strategies:

  1. minimal graphics, navigation elements and “administrative debris”, placing the emphasis on the videos.
  2. an extensive help section with prominent links to it throughout the site.

The logo and navigation bar were made vertically narrow to give the video player breathing room, while the Boy Scout-like color scheme of blue, white and muted gold was chosen for a neutral unobtrusive feel. On the home page, huge unmissable text in the middle of the screen clearly gets across that this isn't a video site like you might be used to.

original home page (July 2007)
logo and motto

To reinforce the interactive nature of the videos, both the motto and the large welcome graphic on the home page incorporated the term “hypervideo”, with a link from the banner text to an explanation and tutorial in the help section. Also, the logo is a large asterisk, a nod towards the similarity between the role of the annotations in the video and footnotes in a book.

The typeface for the company name is FF Meta, a sans-serif originally developed in 1984 for the German Federal Post that implies a bit more class and professionalism than the typical trendy rounded fonts in the “web 2.0” look. The motto is set in Silkscreen, a bitmap font that is easy to read at small sizes and gives a small hint of the advanced tech under the hood.

sidebar, with version and source info

Also unusual was the fact that the sidebar had to get across both that there are multiple versions of each video, as well as the fact that each video was originally from another website altogether. I used a small batch of icons to get across the various states (your video, group videos, and locked versions of other people's videos) as well as prompts for visitors to create their own.

The metadata section was kept short—the intent was to focus attention on the notes in the videos, not peripheral stuff that they could and should find out about elsewhere.

3. usability tests and video tutorials

Before the site launched publically, the product manager and I ran usability tests using a group of local high school students. After analyzing the major obstacles, it became clear that the biggest hurdle for most people (even tech-savvy youth) was not about how to use the video player and attach notes to objects, but understanding that the videos were interactive and realizing that they could do anything other than just watch them.

Based on this, we produced two short videos explaining the unique aspects of the Asterpix player and its advanced object recognition. The screencasts provide a much better introduction than either reading the text-heavy FAQ pages or diving right into watching an Asterpix video.

original screencasts (July 2007)

Summary

One lesson I've taken from this project is that lots of whitespace, big font sizes and plain language is a very opinionated graphic design approach and takes guts to both create and maintain.

2. Paybackable paybackable.com

This was a short project for Kevin Reeth, a friend of a friend and co-founder of Esomnie Software, a Rails consultancy that transitioned into making their own products, much like 37signals did in 2001. Paybackable was one of their first apps, a mileage and expense tracker that aimed to simplify the typically complex process of expense reporting.

Their initial web app gathered acclaim and started building an audience, yet was visually sparse and basically a functional demo for their concept.

before—original home page (Jan 2008)

After talking to Kevin for an afternoon about what pain points they were trying to solve, I agreed to build out a quick redesign of a single page, the public-facing home page, to see if we could focus the branding and design to clearly communicate what they were all about.

after—redesigned home page (Jan 2008)
new logo (Jan 2008)

The logo and header type were set in Diavlo, a new sans-serif that is a bit square and sharp. It does the tough job of communicating both a Hevetica-like professionalism while also injecting some fun and personality, without stooping to the cartoonish level of a MS Comic Sans brush script.

More importantly, I added a clear visual hierarchy, differented the logo from the body content, and highlighted both the main selling points and the registration prompt with giant buttons, all of which was lost in a maze of plain text before.

This project taught me the importance of heuristics—you don't have to test every design in advance in order for it to be an improvement, sometimes you can simply rely on your training, instinct and past experience to create something better.

Update: where are they now?

Unfortunately, I didn't have the time to work on a side project while already working at a startup full-time. Luckily, they found another designer to overhaul Paybackable and the end result is also an improvement from the initial version, plus they've applied the visual treatment to other apps like Bootstrap and unified their product line.

3. Social Media socialmedia.com

Social Media was one of many startups focused almost exclusively on Facebook following the launch of the developer platform in the spring of 2007 and quickly became a strong contender on the back of their massively popular stat-tracking application for developers, reborn from an earlier version on Myspace. Social Media also created apps and made a self-serve ad network, which had Adsense-like self-service and CPC features, unlike the old school banner ads on most other apps.

It was an exciting and fast-moving environment to be in—I made a Facebook app on my first day on the job! Having the machinery in place to make apps quickly meant that we were able to go from scribbling ideas on napkins in the morning to reviewing mockups in the afternoon and then finally handing off to a programming team that built it by the next day.

mockups of the Matchmaker app (fake data to preserve privacy)
Swiss Army Toolbar

One realization I had early on was that the consequence of being able to make so many apps was dispersion—a lot of related apps could benefit from being bundled together and also needed a bit of visual consistency to look like they were made from the same shop. At the same time, it was a possibility that we might start using the same virtual currency across apps.

The solution I came up with was a common toolbar-style header that was customized for each app:

wireframes of the header
example of the header in use

This header wasn't intended just for fun timewasters, it would act as a toolbar for the developers already using Appsholic by showing them a suite of apps related to tracking their traffic and ad spending.
